Brief History:

In 2002, the first year of the Bloomberg Mayoralty, the New York City Public School system shifted from a decentralized governance model to a system of Mayoral Control. Following cities like Boston and Chicago in the 1990âs, NYC Mayoral Control undid the community control model that had been put in place over 40 years ago. The 32 local school boards were abolished, as was the 7-person central Board of Education, and were replaced by a 13-person Panel for Educational Priorities, of which the Mayor has a majority appointment. In 1970, New York City had changed its school governance from a professional control paradigm under centralization to a more lay form of control under decentralization, put in place through elected community school boards and districts. This system of decentralization lasted until the change in law in 2002. Mayoral Control was extended in 2009 by the NYS Senate for 6 more years, and it is set to expire in 2015. At the time, Bloomberg pointed to improvements in standardized test scores and graduation rates as rationale for the continued support of Mayoral Control.

Under the 2009 bill, the Panel for Educational Policy, the oversight board that replaced the old Board of Education and is controlled by the mayor, will decide on every contract of more than $1 million, and the city will be required to hold hearings in local communities before shutting down a school. The cityâs Independent Budget Office, which has long monitored City Hall and City Council programs, will also have the ability to oversee Education Department spending.
